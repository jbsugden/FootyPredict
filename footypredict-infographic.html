1. Data Collection
Fetching real match results and fixtures
▼
FootyPredict pulls live data from two sources: football-data.org (for the Premier League and other major leagues) and the NPL API at thenpl.co.uk (for Northern Premier League divisions). A built-in scheduler checks for new results automatically, or an admin can trigger a sync manually. You can also import historical data via CSV upload.

3. Strength Model
Calculating how good each team's attack and defence are
▼
Before simulating, the system works out how strong each team is. It looks at all their recent results and calculates an attack strength (how many goals they score compared to average) and a defence strength (how many they concede). Recent matches count more than older ones — a team on a hot streak is treated as stronger than their full-season average might suggest.

Poisson Model

Each team gets attack & defence parameters relative to the league average. These become the λ (expected goals) for any matchup.

StrengthCalculator

Time Decay

Matches are weighted by age: 100% (last 6 weeks), 70% (6–12 weeks), 40% (older). This makes the model reactive to form changes.

ELO Ratings

A parallel ELO system (separate from Poisson) tracks each team's overall quality. Uses variable K-factor: K=20 for top leagues, K=40 for non-league.

EloCalculator
attackteam = weighted_goals_scored / league_avg_goals
defenceteam = weighted_goals_conceded / league_avg_goals
λhome = league_avg × attackhome × defenceaway
λaway = league_avg × attackaway × defencehome

The score_probability_matrix() method generates an 11×11 matrix where cell [i,j] = P(home scores i, away scores j), computed as the outer product of two Poisson PMF vectors. The matrix sums to ~1 (truncation at 10 goals loses negligible probability mass for reasonable λ values).

4. Dixon-Coles Correction
Fixing the model's blind spot for low-scoring games
▼
A plain Poisson model gets most scores right, but it's bad at predicting low-scoring games — it under-predicts 0-0 and 1-0 results, and over-predicts 1-1 draws. The Dixon-Coles correction (from a famous 1997 paper) applies a small tweak to these specific scorelines to make the model more accurate.

Correction Factor τ

A multiplicative factor applied only to the four cells (0,0), (1,0), (0,1), (1,1) in the score probability matrix. Controlled by parameter ρ ≈ −0.13.

Effect

0-0 draws: probability increased
1-0, 0-1: slightly reduced
1-1 draws: reduced
Matrix is re-normalised to sum to 1 after correction.

τ(0,0) = 1 − λh · λa · ρ
τ(1,0) = 1 + λa · ρ   |   τ(0,1) = 1 + λh · ρ
τ(1,1) = 1 − ρ

With ρ = −0.13 and typical lambdas (~1.3–1.5), τ(0,0) ≈ 1.23 (boosted), τ(1,1) ≈ 1.13 (also slightly boosted in absolute terms since ρ is negative), and τ(1,0), τ(0,1) < 1 (reduced). A safety guard returns τ=1 if the factor would go negative. The matrix is renormalised after applying corrections to maintain valid probabilities.

5. Monte Carlo Simulation
Playing out the rest of the season 10,000 times
▼
This is the core of FootyPredict. The system takes every remaining fixture in the season and simulates the result of each match using the strength model. It then builds a final league table. It repeats this 10,000 times, with each simulation producing slightly different random scores. By counting how often each team finishes in each position, we get a probability distribution — for example, "Team A has a 34% chance of finishing 1st, 28% chance of 2nd" and so on.

Process

1. Pre-compute λ for all remaining fixtures
2. For each simulation: sample goals from corrected Poisson, build final table
3. Aggregate: count positions, compute mean points

Performance

Goals are sampled via vectorised np.random.Generator.poisson(). Low-scoring outcomes go through Dixon-Coles rejection sampling. Target: 10,000 runs in <10 seconds.

Output

Per team: mean_pos, mean_points, and pos_dist[] — a probability vector where index 0 = P(finish 1st), index 1 = P(finish 2nd), etc.

TeamPrediction

Standings Logic

Each simulated table is ranked by: Points (desc) → Goal Difference (desc) → Goals For (desc). Same rules as real league tables.

for _ in range(10_000):

    sampled_home = rng.poisson(lambdas_home)   # shape: (n_fixtures,)
    sampled_away = rng.poisson(lambdas_away)

    # Dixon-Coles correction for low-scoring games
    for i, fixture in enumerate(remaining_fixtures):
        if sampled_home[i] <= 1 and sampled_away[i] <= 1:
            sampled_home[i], sampled_away[i] = dc_corrected_sample(...)

    # Apply results to a copy of current standings
    sim_standings = deepcopy(current_standings)
    for i, fixture in enumerate(remaining_fixtures):
        apply_result(sim_standings, fixture.home, fixture.away,
                     sampled_home[i], sampled_away[i])

    # Rank and record finishing positions
    ranked = rank_standings(sim_standings)
    for pos, team in enumerate(ranked):
        position_counts[team][pos] += 1

The rejection sampling for Dixon-Coles works by: sampling a score from the independent Poisson, computing τ, then accepting the sample with probability τ. If rejected, resample (up to 20 attempts, then fall back to raw Poisson). This approximates the full corrected distribution without needing to construct and sample from the full joint probability matrix for every fixture.

# Custom filter registered in routes.py
def _top_positions(pos_dist, n=3):
    indexed = list(enumerate(pos_dist))
    indexed.sort(key=lambda x: x[1], reverse=True)
    return indexed[:n]

Glossary

Poisson Distribution A statistical distribution that models the number of events (goals) in a fixed interval, given a known average rate (λ). Perfect for football because goals are relatively rare and roughly independent.
Monte Carlo A technique that uses repeated random sampling to estimate outcomes. Instead of calculating one prediction, we simulate thousands of possible seasons and look at the distribution of results.
Dixon-Coles A refinement to the basic Poisson model (published 1997) that corrects for the correlation between home and away goals in low-scoring matches. Named after its authors, Mark Dixon and Stuart Coles.
ELO Rating A rating system (originally from chess) where teams gain or lose points based on match results. Beating a strong team gives more points than beating a weak one. Used here alongside the Poisson model for an additional quality signal.
λ (Lambda) The expected number of goals a team will score in a match. Computed from their attack strength and the opponent's defence strength. A λ of 1.5 means the team is expected to score about 1.5 goals.
ρ (Rho) The Dixon-Coles correlation parameter, typically around −0.13. Negative values mean that low-scoring draws (0-0) happen more often than the independent Poisson model would predict.
